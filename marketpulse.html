Sector Moves
Energy & Nuclear
NRC's modernized SMR licensing framework is the most significant regulatory development in advanced fission in 20 years

The new technology-neutral review pathway could compress the advanced fission licensing timeline from 8–10 years to 5–6 years for applications that meet the new design criteria. This removes the primary bear case for pre-revenue nuclear names — the single most frequently cited reason to avoid the sector was regulatory timeline risk, not technology. The 90-day comment period is ongoing. Watch for conventional energy and environmental group opposition, which could modify the framework before finalization. If the rule survives largely intact, the deployment clock for the first commercial SMRs moves from "2032–2035 at best" to "2030 possible."

Bullish — advanced fission sector broadly
AI / Software
Enterprise AI moving from pilots to production — hyperscaler capex is the leading indicator

Microsoft, Google, and Meta raised 2026 AI infrastructure spend guidance in Q1 earnings, collectively committing over $350B for the year. This is the clearest signal that hyperscalers see revenue returns justifying the spend — not just competitive positioning. For enterprise AI software, the implication is a growing deployment TAM: more enterprises can now afford to run production AI workloads that require specialized deployment platforms. The sector is entering the second phase of the cycle, where early-adopter revenue transitions to broad enterprise adoption. Regulatory AI governance frameworks emerging in the EU and US could slow certain verticals, but defense and healthcare — the regulated industries most differentiated by specialized platforms — are largely exempt.

Bullish — enterprise AI deployment cycle accelerating
Space
Starship operational capability changes the economics of every large-scale space program — cuts go in two directions

With Starship Flight 9 achieving a fully nominal mission, SpaceX is targeting 10+ launches in 2026, each capable of putting 100+ metric tons to low Earth orbit. This changes the unit economics of satellite constellation deployment (falling launch costs benefit operators), lunar logistics (NASA Artemis program now has a viable heavy-lift option at lower cost), and space infrastructure broadly. The catch: companies that planned their revenue models around Falcon 9 pricing ($6–9K/kg) will need to reprice as Starship drives marginal costs toward $100–500/kg at scale. Pure-play launch competitors face structural headwinds; companies with systems-integration and satellite-manufacturing capabilities benefit from lower launch costs without the competitive risk.

Bullish (customers) — watch launch-competitor exposure
